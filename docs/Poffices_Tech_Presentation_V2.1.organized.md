LLMs don’t “Understand” “Meaning” LLMs are used in NLP , machine translation, and content creation, enhancing productivity.
Learning from a large corpus allows LLMs to understand the meaning of words.
It’s important to note that LLMs don’t really understand anything. They create statistical patterns that groups similar tokens based on a complex measure of how similar or dissimilar they are.

Data used to Train LLMs LLMs are trained in an unsupervised manner on vast quantities of open source and licensed data Responses are refined using question-response pairs (“InstructGPT”) from the web, humans or bootstrapped (i.e. the LLM outputs its own pairs) reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) is used to reward LLMs to give appropriate responses (“guardrails”)

Overview of R.I.S.E. Prompt Model
- The R.I.S.E. prompt model introduces a structured framework to optimize prompt engineering by focusing on four principal components: Role, Instructions/Input, Steps, and Evaluation criteria / Expectation.
- This model helps users systematically construct prompts that steer generative AI towards generating precise and relevant outputs, particularly in educational and professional domains.
- Implementing R.I.S.E. enhances the alignment of AI responses with user goals, ensuring consistency and quality in generative AI applications.
- Example:

The RISE Components Steps (S): Outline the specific instructions or logical sequence the AI should follow to complete the task.
- 1. Analyze competitors.
- 2. Identify 3 unique selling points.
- 3. Draft a LinkedIn ad. ”
Role (R): Define the expertise or persona the AI should adopt.
- "You are an experienced Marketing Strategist specialized in SaaS. "
Expectation (E): Clearly state the desired outcome, target audience, or success criteria
- "Provide a table comparing features and a 150-word ad draft in a professional tone. " Input (I): Provide the necessary data, background, or context the AI needs to work with.
- "Our target audience is HR managers, and our product is a remote-work tracking tool. "

Writing the Role of the Agent Prompt
- To write an effective Role for an AI agent using the RISE framework, you must go beyond simple job titles to define the persona's expertise, perspective, and boundaries. A strong role serves as a "lens" that filters how the AI processes information and determines the tone of its output.
3. Establish Tone and Communication Style
Explicitly name the desired "voice" (e.g., authoritative, empathetic, skeptical) to ensure the AI's personality matches the audience.
Constraint Example: "Maintain a professional but empathetic tone; use clear analogies instead of technical jargon".
4. Use "Identity Guardrails"
Set boundaries on what the agent should or should not do to keep it within its assigned persona.
Instruction: "You must only provide advice based on established business rules. If a question is outside your expertise in SaaS marketing, state that you cannot answer it".
1. Be Hyper-Specific
Avoid generic roles like "Marketing Expert." Instead, provide depth by specifying years of experience, a particular niche, or a specific seniority level.
Vague: "You are a teacher." Specific: "You are a Senior High School Physics Teacher with 15 years of experience in explaining complex thermodynamics to students who struggle with math".
2. Define the "Mission" and Values
Briefly state what the agent's primary goal is and what it values most in its work.
Example: "As a Cybersecurity Auditor, your primary mission is to identify high-risk vulnerabilities. You prioritize factual accuracy and security over conversational flair".

Writing the Input of the Agent Prompt
- In the RISE framework, the Input (I) provides the raw material—the facts, data, and context—the AI needs to perform its task. Without a robust input, the AI often resorts to generic "hallucinations" because it lacks the specific parameters of your situation. To write an effective Input, follow these four strategies:
3. Use Structured Formats
For complex information, present data in bullet points, tables, or JSON. Structured inputs allow the AI to parse relationships between data points more accurately than long paragraphs.
Technique: List your "Must-Haves" and "Must-Nots" explicitly within the input section.
4. Use Dynamic Placeholders
If you are building a reusable template, use placeholders like {{customer_name}} or {{data_source}}. This allows you to quickly swap out the specific data while keeping the framework intact.
1. Provide Situational Context
Explain the specific circumstances or environment where the output will be used.
Poor: "Write a social media post." Effective: "We are launching a new biodegradable phone case for Earth Day. The campaign targets eco-conscious Gen Z in Europe".
2. Supply Data and Facts
Include all necessary background information like product details, research findings, or brand guidelines. If you have extensive data, use delimiters (like """ or ###) to clearly separate the input text from the instructions.

Writing the Step of the Agent Prompt
- In the RISE framework, Steps (S) are at the core of guiding the AI's thinking logic and execution sequence. Breaking down tasks into a series of small instructions can effectively reduce AI "hallucinations" and significantly improve the accuracy of output. Here are four tips for writing efficient steps:
3. Incorporate feedback loops or checkpoints
Include self-auditing commands in steps to ensure that the output meets quality standards.
Example: "After completing your first draft, check that the content includes at least two product benefits and make sure the tone is not too blunt."
4. Clarify branching logic (If-Then)
If the task involves multiple possibilities, give clear paths.
Example: "If the review is positive, write a thank-you note; If it's negative, apologize first and ask specific questions."
1. Mark the logical order with serial numbers
Avoid long paragraphs and use 1. 2. 3. and other digital markers. This forces the AI to follow a step-by-step approach rather than jumping to conclusions all at once.
Example:
- Read the customer reviews I provide.
- Extract the three most frequently mentioned pain points.
- Write a solution for each pain point.
2. Introducing the "chain of thought“
Ask the AI to analyze or explain before giving a final answer.
This is crucial for complex tasks like data analysis or strategy formulation.
Prompt: "Before answering, list the key factors you consider." or "Please explain your inference process step by step."

Writing the Expectation of the Agent Prompt
- To wrap up the framework, the Expectation (E) (sometimes referred to as Evaluation or Exit Criteria) defines exactly what
"success" looks like. It prevents the AI from giving you a response that is too long, too short, or in the wrong format.
- Here is how to master the final letter of the RISE framework:
3. Specify the "Audience" Lens
Remind the AI who the end-user is so it adjusts the complexity of the output.
Example: "The output should be understandable for a 10year-old" vs. "The output should be suitable for a Board of Directors presentation."
4. Quality Control (The "Don'ts")
Explicitly state what to exclude to avoid common AI pitfalls.
Example: "Do not include an introductory 'Here is the information' sentence; start directly with the table."
1. Define the Format and Structure
Tell the AI exactly how to display the information. If you don't specify this, it will usually default to plain paragraphs.
Options: Table, bullet points, Markdown code, JSON, professional email, or a 2-page report summary.
Example: "Present the findings in a Markdown table with columns for 'Risk Factor', 'Impact Level', and 'Mitigation Strategy'."
2. Set Constraints (Length and Tone)
Be strict about boundaries to save yourself from editing later.
Length: "Keep the summary under 100 words" or "Provide at least 5 distinct ideas." Tone: "Use an authoritative yet encouraging tone" or "Avoid using corporate jargon and buzzwords."

What is GenAI Agent? https://youtu.be/FwOTs4UxQS4?si=alB8YYOTFtLhVDMF

A Generative AI (GenAI) Agent is an autonomous system that uses Large Language Models (LLMs) as its "brain" to not only generate content but also toreason, plan, and take actions to achieve specific goals.
Unlike traditional chatbots that only respond to prompts, GenAI agents can independently use external tools, browse the web, and interact with other software to complete complex, multi-step workflows.

Types of GenAI Agents

- Foundation models (LLM, Multimodal models):
Provides the natural language and multimedia processing.
- Tool integration: Provides the external information and functions such as databases, web tools and code execution.
- Agent Core System: Provides memory to store shortterm context (conversation history) and long-term knowledge to maintain consistency and learn from past interactions. reasoning, natural language understanding, and decision-making capabilities.
- Orchestration Layer: The ability to break down a highlevel goal (e.g., Task Management, Agent Evaluation and
Workflow) into smaller, actionable sub-tasks.
- User Interface: Provides interface, external functions and APIs that allow the agent to “interface with users and other agents.
Architecture of GenAI Agents

Three Types of GenAI Agents Basic Agent (LLM + Prompt) Workflow Agent Thinking Agent This is the most basic application level, focusing on the encapsulation of knowledge.
- Core Mechanics: Define your character's personality, limitations, and areas of expertise through granular System Prompts.
- How it works: User input -> LLMs respond based on prompts. It primarily relies on the model's built-in knowledge or simple RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation).
- Limitations: It does not have the ability to "act" and cannot actively manipulate external tools, but can only provide information within dialog boxes.
- Example: A chatbot set up as a "senior legal assistant" that specializes in explaining contract terms.

Three Types of GenAI Agents This level introduces standardized processes with a focus on reliability and automation.
- Core mechanism: Break down tasks into predefined steps (DAGs, directed acyclic graphs). The developer presets "what to do in the first step, and if the result is A, then perform step two" .
- How it works: LLMs perform specific tasks at each default node. This is often combined with program logic such as LangGraph or n8n.
- Features: Although tools can be called (such as sending emails and checking data), the path is planned and the behavior is highly predictable, suitable for enterprise-level fixed business processes.
- Example: Automated customer service system - first identify semantics, transfer manual if it is a complaint, and if it is an order check, go to the database to grab data and reply.

Introduction to Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
- Tech Training Lecture Note: GenAI Agent Technologies
Poffices.AI By Ir Prof Alan Lam, JP

- RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is a framework that optimizes the output of large language models (LLMs) by allowing AI to retrieve relevant information from externally trusted knowledge bases before generating responses.
- It's like conducting an "openbook exam": while traditional AI answers questions from memory, RAG AI consults reference books and organizes answers based on the content in the book.
What is Retrieval-Augmented Generation?

A typical RAG system encompasses the following key steps:
1. Data Indexing: Breaking down raw data (such as
PDFs, web pages, manuals) into chunks and converting them into numerical vectors that computers can understand, which are stored in a vector database.
2. Retrieval: When a user asks a question, the database searches for the most semantically relevant blocks of information.
3. Augmentation: Merge the retrieved information with the user's original question to construct a new prompt full of contextual information.
4. Generation: The LLM generates precise and factbased responses based on this enhanced prompt.

Ir Prof Alan Lam, JP RAG solves three major pain points of traditional LLMs:
- Reduce Hallucinations: Forcing AI to speak based on facts rather than fabricating plausible misinformation.
- Real-Time Knowledge Updates:
Instead of spending a lot of money to retrain models, simply update external databases to get the latest information (such as current stock prices or the latest company policies).
- Traceability: AI can provide citations or links to data sources, allowing users to verify the accuracy of information.

The core workflow typically follows these four major phases: (4) (4.1.1) General-purpose lexicons are dictionaries of words annotated with sentiment polarity values indicating positive, negative, or neutral sentiment. (4.4) Human-in-the-loop (HITL) integrates human expertise into the automated sentiment analysis pipeline to improve model accuracy, especially in challenging cases.
The HITL process involves:

Human review: Experts verify or correct outputs, particularly for ambiguous texts.

- Subtle emotional cues,
- Ethical or sensitive contexts.
HITL balances scalability with quality assurance, increasing trustworthiness of sentiment analysis applications.

What is reasoning in AI?
- Reasoning in artificial intelligence (AI) refers to the mechanism of using available information to generate predictions, make inferences and draw conclusions. It involves representing data in a form that a machine can process and understand, then applying logic to arrive at a decision.
- Recent releases of reasoning models, including DeepSeek-R1, Google’s Gemini 2.0
Flash Thinking, IBM’s Granite 3.2 and OpenAI’s o1 series and o3-mini, have put AI reasoning in the spotlight.
- Advancements in AI have allowed its capabilities to evolve from following predefined rules to integrating some form of reasoning. And with AI adoption increasing, the role of the technology is shifting.
Rather than just generating answers, today’s reasoning models can reflect on and break down their analysis step-by-step. This allows AI to tackle ever more complex problems, guiding users to take meaningful action.
However, AI reasoning is not a recent capability and has been programmed into AI since its earliest days, according to IBM Research Fellow Francesca Rossi.
Preprogrammed reasoning skills gave AI models’ predictions a degree of certainty that might be trusted and relied upon. But newer AI models might lack that certainty and reliability due to their more dynamic reasoning capabilities, Rossi said. And while AI reasoning is designed to mimic human reasoning, Rossi noted that AI still needs much work to truly reason like humans do.

How reasoning in AI works
- Reasoning in AI is depicted as a system typically made up of two core components:
- ● Knowledge base
- ● Inference engine
- Knowledge base
- The knowledge base is the backbone of an AI reasoning system. It contains knowledge graphs, ontologies, semantic networks and other models of knowledge representation. These structured forms map realworld entities—such as concepts, domain-specific information, events, facts, objects, relationships, rules and situations—into a structure that AI models can process and understand.
- Inference engine
- Acting as the brain of an AI reasoning system is the inference engine. It’s powered by trained machine learning models. The inference engine implements the needed logic and reasoning methods to analyze data from the knowledge base and reach a decision.

How reasoning in AI works
- AI reasoning in action
- To illustrate how an AI reasoning system works, let’s take an autonomous robotic floor cleaner as an example. Its knowledge base can contain information about different kinds of floors and what type of cleaning they require. - Environment: The set of conditions or space within which agents operate. It could be physical (robotics, IoT) or virtual (knowledge bases, simulation environments).
- Communication: Agents communicate via direct messages or indirect environmental changes. Communication protocols define message formats and interaction sequences.

- Hierarchical MAS, featuring layered control structures with supervisory agents.
- Heterogeneous MAS, where agents differ significantly in function and complexity.

Overview of GenAI Workflow Agents GenAI Workflow Agents, also referred to as agentic AI, are autonomous artificial intelligence systems designed to perform complex workflows independently.
The fundamental characteristic that distinguishes these agents is their ability to act autonomously within dynamic and unstructured environments, rather than merely reacting based on predefined rules or static data.

Key functionalities of GenAI Workflow Agents
- Decision-Making Based on Context: Agents assess changing environments and user contexts to determine the best course of action.
- Goal Decomposition: Complex goals are broken down into manageable subtasks, which the agent autonomously pursues in a sequenced or parallel manner.
- Collaboration with Tools and Systems:Agents can interact with external applications, APIs, databases, and other AI systems to gather information and execute tasks.
- Reflection and Adaptation: By monitoring their own performance and environmental changes, agents improve outcomes through iterative learning.

Components of GenAI Workflow Agents
- Perception Module: This component enables the agent to perceive and interpret inputs from its environment, including user commands, external data sources, and real-time sensor data if applicable. It leverages techniques such as natural language processing (NLP) to understand textual or spoken input.
- Cognitive Engine: Powered often by Large Language Models (LLMs) or other advanced AI models, this engine processes information, performs reasoning, and plans actions. It decides how to decompose goals, sequences tasks, and adapts plans according to feedback.
- Action/Execution Layer: This layer interfaces with tools, APIs, databases, and other systems to execute tasks. It performs operations such as querying databases, manipulating software tools, or initiating communication.
- Memory and Learning Module: Agents maintain an internal state memory to store relevant context and track progress. Reinforcement learning and continuous feedback support ongoing improvements.
- Supervision and Coordination: Some architectures involve a

238 Ir Prof Alan Lam, JP Design Principles of Advanced Agents
- Interleaving Reasoning and Acting: Rather than segregating thought processes from execution, the agent alternates between ‘thoughts’ (verbalized steps of chain-of-thought reasoning) and ‘actions’ (tool use, API calls, information acquisition). This design enables continuous feedback and dynamic strategic adjustment, closely replicating human problem-solving and decision-making.
- Language as the Medium for Coordination: Using natural language to represent reasoning steps allows the LLM to structure complex tasks into coherent, sequential subtasks that are accessible and interpretable. This linguistic framework makes it easier to trace decision chains, debug agent behavior, and integrate with external knowledge bases and APIs through prompt engineering.
- Tool-Augmented Reasoning: The agent is furnished with predefined tools, such as bash commands, text editors, or APIs, that can be invoked to gather information or perform specific tasks in the environment. This tripartite interaction—between the language model’s reasoning capacity, the contextual prompt, and external tool invocation—enhances the agent’s functional scope and precision.
- Iterative Task Execution with State Retention: The ReAct agent operates in a loop that persists until completion criteria are met, allowing multiple attempts to refine outputs based on intermediate scoring or feedback. Memory retention through message history compaction preserves relevant context, enabling handling of tasks that extend beyond the LLM’s standard context window.
- Customizability and Extensibility: Developers can adjust the agent’s behavior by modifying prompt structures, callbacks, scoring mechanisms, and tool integrations to suit specific task requirements or domains. This flexibility is essential for educational applications, where different subjects or learning stages may call for tailored AI assistance.

- Toolset Module: A configurable suite of domain-relevant tools such as shell command interpreters, text editors, web search APIs, or domain-specific utilities that the agent can utilize to perform actionable tasks in the environment.

Reference of Theory of Vector RAG At the core of Vector RAG lies modeling knowledge as a directed and attributed graph 𝐺 = (𝑉, 𝐸, 𝒜), where:
- 𝑉 is a set of nodes representing entities or concepts
- 𝐸 ⊆ 𝑉 × 𝑉 is a set of edges representing relationships between nodes
- 𝒜 assigns attributes or labels to nodes and edges
1. Entity and Relation Embedding
Each node 𝑣 ∈ 𝑉 and edge 𝑒 ∈ 𝐸 can be embedded into a continuous vector space to enable retrieval and graph traversal: 𝐡𝑣 = Embed 𝑣 , 𝐡𝑒 = Embed(𝑒). These embeddings capture semantic information to support similarity comparisons and ranking.
2. Query Representation
User query 𝑞 is processed and converted into a vector representation: 𝐡𝑞 = Encode(𝑞). This vector represents the intent or concept of the query in the same embedding space as entities and relations.
3. Graph-Based Retrieval Score
For retrieval, a scoring function 𝑠(𝑞, 𝑣) evaluates the relevance of a node 𝑣 to query 𝑞:
𝑠(𝑞, 𝑣) = sim(𝐡𝑞, 𝐡𝑣) + ෍ 𝑢∈𝒩(𝑣) 𝑤𝑢𝑣 ⋅ rel(𝑢, 𝑣) Where:
- sim(⋅) denotes a similarity metric, such as cosine similarity.
- 𝒩(𝑣) denotes the neighbors of node 𝑣.
- 𝑤𝑢𝑣 is a learned weight for the edge from 𝑢 to 𝑣.
- rel(𝑢, 𝑣) captures relationship strength or type embedding.
This formula integrates node similarity with the context from related nodes via the graph edges, enhancing retrieval by relational context. 1 2 3

Reference of Theory of GraphRAG
4. Multi-Hop Reasoning
Vector RAG supports multi-hop reasoning, where the relevance score is propagated through multiple edges.
Formally, for 𝑘-hop neighbors:
𝑠(𝑘)(𝑞, 𝑣) = 𝑠(𝑞, 𝑣) + 𝛾 ෍ 𝑢∈𝒩(𝑣) 𝑠(𝑘−1) (𝑞, 𝑢) Here, 𝛾 is a decay factor ensuring that distant nodes contribute less but are not ignored. This mechanism allows the system to infer indirect relationships.
5. Graph Augmented Answer Generation
Once relevant nodes and edges are retrieved, the generative model (e.g., an LLM) is conditioned on the retrieved subgraph information 𝑆 ⊆ 𝐺:
ො𝑎 = argmax 𝑎 𝑃(𝑎 ∣ 𝑞, 𝑆) Where ො𝑎 is the generated answer, 𝑎 is any possible answer, and 𝑃(⋅) is modeled by the generative language model.
6. Query Expansion and Rewriting
To better utilize the graph, query preprocessing includes steps like entity extraction ℰ(𝑞) and expansion:
𝑞′= 𝑞 + expand(ℰ(𝑞)) This augmented query 𝑞′ improves graph matching and retrieval.
These formulas form the basic mathematical foundation of Vector RAG systems, combining vector embeddings, graph structures, and generative models for retrieval-augmented generation. 64 5

- The LLM reads each Context Chunk.
- Determines whether the chunk is relevant to the query.
- Generates an intermediate answer (R) and assigns it a relevance score (0–100).
3. Reduce (Aggregation):
- Discard all answers with a score of 0.
- Sort the remaining answers by score.
- Merge all intermediate answers to produce the final global response.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG Knowledge Graph Construction forms the backbone of the GraphRAG framework. It is the process of creating a graphbased data structure which captures entities as nodes and their semantic or relational connections as edges. This structured graph allows the system to represent complex, multi-relational data that is interpretable by LLMs.
Mathematically, a knowledge graph is defined as:
𝐺 = (𝑉, 𝐸, ℒ) where 𝑉 = {𝑣1, 𝑣2, . . . , 𝑣𝑛} is the set of nodes representing entities, 𝐸 ⊆ 𝑉 × 𝑉 is the set of edges representing relationships, ℒ is a labeling function assigning types or attributes to nodes and edges.
The construction involves several steps:
- Entity Extraction: Extract key entities 𝑣𝑖 from unstructured data using Named Entity Recognition (NER) and other NLP techniques.
- Relation Extraction: Identify relationships between pairs of entities, representing these as edges 𝑒𝑖𝑗 = (𝑣𝑖, 𝑣𝑗).
- Attribute Annotation: Assign labels or attributes to nodes and edges reflecting types, weights, or timestamps.
- Graph Enrichment: Incorporate external ontologies or domain knowledge to augment the constructed graph.
For example, given a document corpus, the entities “Albert Einstein” and “Theory of Relativity” are extracted as nodes, and a “developed by” relationship forms an edge between them.
The adjacency matrix 𝐴 representing the graph 𝐺 is defined as 𝐴𝑖𝑗 = ቊ1 if (𝑣𝑖, 𝑣𝑗) ∈ 𝐸 0 otherwise The adjacency matrix is essential for graph-based computations like clustering and modularity optimization.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG Co-occurrence frequency measures how often two entities appear together within a certain context such as a document, sentence, or paragraph. This statistical measure is foundational in creating weighted edges in the knowledge graph, influencing graph topology and retrieval quality.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG Leiden Graph Clustering Algorithm for Maximizing Modularity Community detection or clustering divides a graph into groups of nodes (communities) that have higher edge densities within groups than between them. The Leiden algorithm is an advanced method for community detection optimizing modularity, widely used in GraphRAG to identify semantically cohesive subgraphs.

- 𝐴 is the adjacency matrix,
- 𝑚 = 1
2 σ𝑖,𝑗 𝐴𝑖𝑗 is the total number of edges,
- 𝑘𝑖 = σ𝑗 𝐴𝑖𝑗 is the degree of node 𝑖,
- 𝛿(𝐶𝑖, 𝐶𝑗) = 1 if nodes 𝑖 and 𝑗 are in the same community, else 0.
Modularity measures if there are more edges inside communities than expected by chance, with values typically between -1 and 1. Higher values mean better community structure.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG Leiden Algorithm The Leiden algorithm improves upon prior methods like Louvain by guaranteeing communities are well-connected and resolving disconnected components within communities.
The algorithm proceeds iteratively:
Local Moving Phase: Moves individual nodes to different communities to locally increase modularity.
Refinement Phase: Splits communities that are not internally connected.
Aggregation Phase: Builds an aggregated network where communities become super-nodes and repeats steps.
The mathematical goal is to find partition 𝑃∗ that maximizes modularity 𝑄:
𝑃∗ = argmax 𝑃 𝑄(𝑃) The Leiden algorithm efficiently approximates this solution.
Leiden Algorithm for Q Optimization Beyond maximizing modularity 𝑄, the Leiden algorithm also optimizes quality functions related to clustering, such as the generalized Potts model or stability metrics.
The optimization problem can be abstracted as maximizing a quality function:
𝑄 = ෍ 𝑐∈𝑃 𝑒𝑐 − 𝛾𝑎𝑐2 where: - 𝑒𝑐 is the fraction of edges inside community 𝑐, - 𝑎𝑐 is the fraction of ends of edges inside community 𝑐, - 𝛾 is a resolution parameter controlling community size sensitivity.
For the resolution parameter 𝛾 = 1, it corresponds to classical modularity; varying 𝛾 allows multi-resolution clustering.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG The Leiden algorithm's optimization iteratively refines partitions to improve 𝑄 via smart heuristics:
- Node movements to communities yielding highest 𝑄 improvement.
- Community splitting to prevent badly connected communities.
- Iterative aggregation to manage hierarchical levels.
Mathematically, the change in 𝑄 when moving node 𝑖 into community 𝐶, 𝛥𝑄𝑖→𝐶, is computed and the maximal gain movement selected.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG Community Detection and Summarization Community detection refers to identifying groups of nodes with dense internal connections. Following clustering with Leiden, GraphRAG employs community summarization to distill each cluster's core knowledge, reducing complex graphs into interpretable summaries for LLM consumption.
Community Detection Formally, from graph 𝐺 = (𝑉, 𝐸), the goal is partition:
𝑃 = {𝐶1, 𝐶2, . . . , 𝐶𝑘} such that each 𝐶𝑖 ⊆ 𝑉, 𝐶𝑖 ∩ 𝐶𝑗 = ∅ for 𝑖 ≠ 𝑗, and communities maximize modularity or other quality functions.
Detection benefits from spectral clustering, modularity maximization (Leiden), or label propagation algorithms.

Extract representative nodes 𝑅𝑐 ⊂ 𝐶, using centrality measures, e.g., PageRank:
𝑃𝑅(𝑣𝑖) = 1 − 𝑑 𝑁 + 𝑑 ෍ 𝑣𝑗∈𝐼𝑛(𝑣𝑖) 𝑃𝑅(𝑣𝑗) 𝑘𝑗 where 𝑑 is damping factor, 𝑁 total nodes.
Generate textual summaries by aggregating salient node and edge information weighted by properties like frequency and centrality.
Create hierarchical summaries by iterating summarization at different clustering levels.
Such summaries facilitate efficient retrieval providing concise yet comprehensive answers, crucial for multi-turn dialogue and query-focused summarization.

Summary of Theory of GraphRAG Map-Reduce Global Search GraphRAG often supports enormous knowledge bases requiring scalable retrieval algorithms. Map-Reduce global search distributes query processing over clusters and integrates partial results efficiently.

Map phase: Parallel local searches over graph partitions or communities to identify relevant nodes/subgraphs.
